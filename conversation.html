Conversation Means Collaboration and Understanding

In many processes, the design of a project is presented as a pre-developed workflow. The requirements and end-goals are listed from the very beginning. User Story Mapping wants to turn each development task into a short, constructive conversation. This gives everyone the opportunity to learn about the work being done, and to have input on how it can be improved, or more easily accomplished.

The psychological effects of Story Mapping are also relevant. Team members feel a stronger sense of ownership and investment in the project, since they actually have an impact on the way things are designed, and because they more thoroughly understand how everything ties together. The engagement level is much higher than that of a simple meeting, where one person speaks to a group whose only goal is to absorb information. By giving everyone an opportunity to voice their concerns, the project is refined and the work is prioritized by a diverse team.

On Coming to Conclusions

Making sure that each team member is on the same page with regards to their expectations for the project is a major concern. By promoting the idea that everyone is free to ask questions and give their input, inconsistencies can be caught and correct before they cause problems. In scenarios where one person is doing all the speaking, each person may have constructed their own view of the project that doesn't mesh with that of the others.

Relics of Conversation

Visualization of your problems and goals is extremely helpful, but visual aids should never replace conversation. Instead they should invoke memories of these sessions, by displaying the most important points and hinting at the tasks to be completed. Without conversation, there is no true understanding, no any process of refinement. The true purpose of these visuals is the organization of ideas.
